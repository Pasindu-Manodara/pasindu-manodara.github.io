Interactive simulations are powerful tools for experiential and embodied learning, yet they remain largely handcrafted and domain-specific. Creating such systems typically requires manually translating conceptual knowledge into executable models, limiting scalability and reuse across domains. We argue that simulations should instead be \emph{compiled from conceptual content}, automatically transforming text, diagrams, or structured explanations into parameterized, manipulable environments.

This position paper proposes a research agenda for \emph{stateful generative environments} that combine generative model synthesis with closed-loop adaptation. We introduce a domain-agnostic abstraction that represents conceptual content as executable models consisting of objects, state variables, manipulable parameters, and dynamic update rules. These models can be compiled into interactive simulation environments, including XR settings, where users explore system behavior by manipulating parameters. Beyond one-shot generation, such environments can leverage multimodal sensing to infer user interaction states and adapt simulations to guide exploration. We outline the architectural foundations required to realize this vision and identify open challenges in structured content extraction, dynamic consistency, cross-domain generalization, and on-device adaptive inference. Treating interactive environments as compiled knowledge artifacts opens a new direction for generative, sensor-aware, and domain-agnostic simulation systems.
